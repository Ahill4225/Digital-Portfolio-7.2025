Mastery-Based Learning: Self-Pacing Plan

This section summarizes the self-pacing plan designed to support young learners in achieving mastery through consistent, individualized practice.

Self-Pacing Interval

Students will self-pace week by week for at least 15 to 30 minutes per day as part of their independent learning and practice after individual or small group instruction. This interval is chosen because young learners need daily consistent time to practice skills for mastery. Daily exit tickets provide data for lesson design, and Google Classroom helps students track assignments and feedback. A 5-day week allows for regular check-ins to ensure students are on a similar trajectory before moving to the next week.

Activity Prioritization & Differentiation (DOK Levels)

Activities are prioritized using a "Must Do," "Should Do," and "Aspire to Do" framework, linked to Depth of Knowledge (DOK) levels to ensure appropriate challenge and engagement.

  • DOK Level 1: Recall and Reproduction (Must Do)

    Focus: Recognizing familiar rhyming words from songs, stories, and poems.

    Example: Playing a rhyming game where students listen to a song and raise their hand every time they hear a rhyming word.

  • DOK Level 2: Skills and Concepts (Should Do)

    Focus: Identifying rhyming words from a group and understanding why they rhyme.

    Example: Students work in pairs to pick rhyming pairs from picture cards and explain why they rhyme.

  • DOK Level 3: Strategic Thinking (Aspire to Do)

    Focus: Generating their own rhyming words and using them in simple sentences or stories.

    Example: Creating a short poem using words that rhyme or making up a word that rhymes with a given word.

Student Progress Tracking

Student progress is tracked through a daily clipboard sheet, Google Classroom gradebook, and required assignment submissions. This provides opportunities for students and parents to view work and teacher feedback. Whole group, small group, and individual trackers are used, with a whole group tracker on a bulletin board for peer support and individual trackers in Google Classroom for consistent accessibility.

Collaboration Strategies

  • Structured Collaboration: Students work with partners or in groups at learning stations using choice boards and Google Slides to organize tasks. A "Choice Board: Recognize and Produce Rhyming Words" guides activities like Rhyme Hunt, Rhyming Song, and Picture Match.
  • Organic Collaboration: The choice board is available on paper and digitally. Students who have shown mastery are listed on a bulletin board and can be chosen by others for support. Strategies like organizing students in pairs for discourse and using anchor charts foster independent and peer support.

Motivation Strategies

  • For Students Falling Behind: Differentiation ideas specific to the unit are used, such as providing picture cards, rhyming word bingo, small group focused attention, and practice on electronic platforms (I-Ready, IXL).
  • For Students Moving Quickly: Choice boards and extension ideas are provided. Advanced students are encouraged to write rhyming poems, play "I Spy" with rhymes, or create rhyming books. Interests like Fortnite are tapped into for engaging activities such as a "Rhyme Battle."
  • Addressing Challenges: An annual interest inventory helps select content and design lessons connected to student interests, such as a "Sprunki Song Sing-Along" for students who like Sprunki characters.

Student Reflection

Students reflect on their learning through exit tickets (paper and/or Google Classroom) and an SEL Check-in created in Google Forms. The SEL check-in includes questions about feelings, sleep, breakfast, and a daily content-specific question (e.g., "write 2 rhyming words"). Menti is also considered for future use.

Blended Learning Instruction Plan

This section showcases the blended learning approach, integrating instructional videos and diverse resources to support the learning objective: CCSS.ELA-Literacy.RF.K.2.a Recognize and produce rhyming words.

Instructional Video: Recognize and Produce Rhyming Words

The video covers the learning objective: "Student is able to produce words that rhyme." It introduces rhyming words, explains their importance for reading, and provides interactive practice using body parts as examples (nose/rose, hand/band, head/bed, hair/chair, ear/cheer). Students are guided to use work mats and cards to match rhyming words and then draw or write rhyming words on a worksheet.

Student Engagement with Videos

To keep students engaged, they use physical visuals (laminated rhyming word cards) from the video during playback to share answers. These cards can also be used at stations/centers for individual practice. A printable worksheet is provided for students to draw or write rhyming words after watching.

Example Worksheet:

How to Watch Videos Guide

A simple guide helps students engage effectively with instructional videos:

  • Before you watch… Find your seat, put on headphones, have video tools (word/picture cards) ready.
  • While you watch… Pause to choose word/picture cards when prompted, rewind if needed, watch again if necessary.
  • After you watch… Ask 3 before me if questions remain, go to Google Classroom for today's assignment, complete the Exit Ticket for the video.

Alternate Instructional Resources

  • Craft Stick Game (Identifying Rhyming Words): Students use craft sticks with happy/sad faces to indicate if two spoken words rhyme (e.g., "car - tar" or "right - wrong"). This activity reinforces auditory discrimination.
  • Rhyming Books and Poems: Students identify rhyming words while listening to or reading stories/poems. They can replace words with rhyming alternatives or circle rhyming words in laminated texts. Examples include "Brush, Brush, Brush Your Teeth," "Scrub-a Dub, Dub," "I’m a Little Teapot," and "Hickory Dickory Dock."

Instructional Strategies

  • One-on-One Instruction: Data determines who needs one-on-one time, which is scheduled on daily slides.
  • Small-Group Instruction: Daily small group instruction is built into the schedule during center rotations, based on assessment data and student needs. Choice boards and lessons are integrated into daily centers.
  • Whole-Class Instruction: Used for introducing concepts, structured mini-lessons, creating anchor chart, or sharing videos. It also reinforces big ideas based on student data, through reteaching, sharing examples, or using tools like IXL group jam to review a skill so all students can examine a question develop a shared understanding through discussion what the best choice and why. The consensus mat strategy is also employed.

Culmination Lesson: Recognizing Rhymes

This section summarizes the culmination lesson focused on demonstrating understanding of spoken words, syllables, and sounds, specifically recognizing and producing rhyming words (CCSS.ELA-Literacy.RF.K.2.a).

Learning Objective & Student-Facing Objective

Learning Objective: Students will learn to identify and recognize rhyming words by listening to and creating rhymes to determine whether a word rhymes.

Student-Facing Objective: "We will learn how to find words that sound the same at the end, like 'cat' and 'hat.'"

The lesson emphasizes that recognizing rhyming words is about hearing and confirming a rhyme, a more passive and auditory skill compared to actively identifying them.

Culmination Lesson Video

This video provides further insights into the culmination lesson, focusing on recognizing rhymes and demonstrating understanding of spoken words and sounds.

Mastery Checks

  • Given 2 pictures, identify if the 2 words rhyme.
  • Given 2 words, identify which 2 words rhyme.
  • Recreate Rhyming Practice 1 as Mastery Check (sample provided).

Learner Practice Activities

  • Lesson 1: Rhyme Time Listening Game: Students give thumbs up/down for rhyming pairs, supported by picture cards.
  • Lesson 2: Rhyming Picture Match: Students work in pairs to match pictures of rhyming words.
  • Lesson 3: Rhyming Word Hopscotch: Students physically identify rhyming pairs by hopping on a grid with rhyming words.
  • Lesson 4: Rhyme Sorting Station: Students sort word cards into rhyming families.
  • Lesson 5: Create-a-Rhyme Book: Students generate and illustrate rhyming words in a booklet format.
